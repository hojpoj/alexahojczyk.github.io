Plex - Search Improvement

Project Overview

Focused on the Discover product team at Plex, I led a majority of the UX strategy and research efforts over the past couple years. One of the major projects for this team was centered around Plex’s Discover feature, which allows a user to search and browse across all their streaming services in one place. In addition to show tracking, search improvements were done in conjunction in order to better serve both PMS and non-PMS users with more relevant search results. A majority of the project focused around low fidelity wireframes across platforms to fine tune an intuitive process. The search improvements created from this project are in current state Plex.

Target Users

Everyone who uses search, especially PMS users who have added functionality for full page search.

Deliverables Created

  • Low fidelity wireframes created in Figma/Figjam
  • User research plans, synthesis reports, and recommendations
  • Usability testing around show tracking concepts to confirm placement, terminology, and flows
  • UX analysis of current Discover flows within Plex
  • Accessibility recommendations/considerations

Main Challenges

  • Because PMS users have a lot more functionality than non-PMS users do with search, it was important to map out flows for each user type and make sure the design didn’t look wonky when all PMS features were not displayed
  • In conjunction with all the work going on for the Discover team, the Streaming Content team was working on a revamp to genres, giving them more functionality around Plex, including letting users search by genre; this use case had to be integrated into the final designs we came up with for Discover

Design Process

Below is a snapshot of each step of the process conducted in order to complete this project.

  • Requirements Gathering

    Meeting with stakeholders to understand problem and business/product needs

  • Non-User Research

    Competitive analyses, heuristic analyses, metadata analyses, etc

  • Wireframing /Blueprinting

    Iterative wireframing to establish flows and functionality across platforms

  • Usability Testing

    Confirming flows with users

  • Iterative Prototyping

    Passed off wires to designer, iteratively usability tested and wireframed when improvements/different path were needed

The Full Process

Requirements Gathering

The need for this project came from consistent feedback we had been getting from users about how search wasn't the greatest. Users wanted more functionality such as genre search, they thought that the results given weren't that relevant to what they had originally searched, they thought some of the text wasn't readable and that the posters were too small, etc. Search is a major feature for Plex, not just because search is important for any streaming service, but also because Plex had universal search; you're able to search across your personal media content, Plex's content, and content from all other streaming services on the market. My product manager approached me to help evaluate and improve search and so, search improvements started.

We had a fairly clear baseline of what we wanted to do based off of user feedback, but starting off, my product team and I decided to focus mainly on our web platforms (Plex has two web platforms: watch.plex.tv and app.plex.tv). Feature-wise, we wanted to focus on genre search, search results display (both for quick search and full page results), and filtering.

Non-User Research

In order to establish a baseline of what search is like across platforms, I did a competitive analysis of 10 different streaming services to figure out the silimarities and differences between search functionality on their web apps as well as their TV apps (TV would be the next focus after we had a firm blueprint for web). Once I had a good understanding of the baseline search functionality, I met with product to see if there was anything in particular that they liked/didn't like from competitors that we could incorporate into our version of search.

From establishing that baseline, I did my own due dilligence of mapping out the current state search for Plex to understand if there were any inconsistencies, the areas where users were fumbling/unhappy with, and overall, all the features that we currently had that we needed to decide if we needed to carry over or get rid of because we didn't have enough usage of them for users to find them valuable.

Wireframing/Blueprinting

I started out by breaking apart the flow into four pieces: quick search, quick search results, full page results, and filtering on full page results (from previous user research, it was decided that we would remove filtering from quick search as 95% of users completely ignored it, the other 5% didn't ever use it, but acknowledged it existed).

Quick search and quick search results were probably the least complicated piece of the puzzle. I was able to split these into the formatting of the results of movies, TV shows (search only goes to the show level, not season or episode), and genres (new functionality) and the overall design of search/results. Full page resutls was a bit more difficult as current state sectioned everything out by location (i.e. Media Libary (if applicable), Live TV, Live TV Tuner (if applicable), On Plex, etc) which led to a lot of scrolling, unnecessary whitespace, and segmentation of results. I ended up coming up with three variations, one with the segmentation in tact and two with a condensed version of results. The condensed version of results also had the addition challenge of figuring out what to do with the poster/image previews for each item as they varied between each content type.

Filtering was the most complicated piece mainly because of 1.) the granularity of what we were capable of getting down to vs the granularity we wanted to get down to and 2.) what we wanted to be able to refine results by (i.e. genre, director, length, location, etc).

Usability Testing and Iterative Prototyping

Once initial wireframes were in a good state, I reviewed them with product, engineering, and design. We went through a few iterations before getting to a happy place and then handed designs off to the UI/UX designer I was working with on the project. As they worked on the high fidelity versions of web, I was working on the TV version of search. The web prototype would eventually be tested to confirm flow, functionality, and terminology, the results from which were applied to the TV prototype in the applicable areas.

Search improvements from this project are in current state on app.plex.tv and on Plex's TV apps.

AVOD Brainstorming Workshop

The FAST and AVOD brainstorming workshops were formatted similarly with affinity mapping, creative matrices, and voting on ideas. Specifically for the AVOD workshop, participants started with a warm-up activity to step into the shoes of the user by recalling as many titles from the main pages of Plex as possible. We then moved onto affinity mapping, where participants were sorted through existing challenges they saw with AVOD (through personal experience and user research), dividing them into groups and then, as a group, coming up with How Might We statements based off of them. From there, we used those HMW statements for our creative matrix and then voted on items via Monopoly money. Shown below are the affinity mapping and creative matrix activities.

AVOD Prioritization Session

Items from the AVOD workshop were pulled into the matrix for prioritization. Following the initial session, I broke out the items into different sections of where research was needed, what we needed more info on in general, what were mainly dev-focused items, what were items were already got started on from a design perspective, and miscellaous, sorting each column by priority order.
