Deloitte - CDPH Work

Project Overview

Within the state of California, I led all user experience and user interfaces efforts over the past two years within 10 major projects. These projects were all covered within one of the largest engagements with California. During this time, we released two new major applications that covered over 20,000+ users as well as maintained and improved the user interface and user experience within multiple pre-existing applications which affected over 500,000 users at any given time.

*** Due to the sensitive nature of the projects, full apps cannot be shown.

Target Users

For FAST: General users (with and without accounts), PMS users with tuners

For AVOD: General, non-PMS users (with and without accounts)

Deliverables Created

  • Low fidelity wireframes created in Sketch
  • Mid/High fidelity wireframes created in Sketch
  • User research plans
  • Usability reports and analyses based off of user testing results
  • User personas for major user groups
  • HTML/CSS/SASS templated for development
  • Accessibility recommendations/considerations
  • Report creation

Main Challenges

  • Because of the nature of most applications, access to users was difficult to gain for in-person user testing
  • Many clients were both clients and users of the systems which led to bias
  • The tools and frameworks used to develop applications were very restrictive in terms of functionality that would to the best UX and UI

Design Process

Below is a snapshot of each step of the process conducted in order to complete this project.

  • Requirements Gathering

    Meeting with stakeholders to understand problem and business/product needs

  • Non-User Research

    Competitive analyses, heuristic analyses, metadata analyses, etc

  • User Research

    Qualitative usability studies to walk through and evaluate current state

  • Synthesis & Recommendations

    Putting everything together to form the whole picture and where to go

  • Brainstorming & Backlog

    Working with stakeholders to brainstorm new features to improve apps

The Full Process

Requirements Gathering

The beginning of both of these project started as a pretty ambiguous "hey, let's fix FAST/AVOD" from my product managers so the first step was to sit down and figure out the who, what, when, where, and why. I set up a meeting with my key stakeholders to figure out the problem(s) they were wanting to solve, metrics they wanted to target, any target users they had in mind, and a timeline.

  • For FAST: My product managers wanted to tackle two main problems: the lack of consistency of the FAST experience across all supported platforms (the platforms of focus ended up being iOS, Android, watch.plex (web), app.plex (web), Fire TV, Roku, and LG/Lightning. The process was going to be start with identifying what's actually happening in FAST, if it aligns with best practices in the industry, figure out how to bring it to a consistent state across platforms that users would be happy with, and then start figuring out areas of improvement for future iterations of FAST.
  • For AVOD: The same problems as above were identified, but for a user group, we focused a lot less on PMS users and more on general users in order to improve the experience to focus more on retention and registration.

Non-User Research

After the initial meeting with stakeholders, I went onto non-user/market research. For this round of research, I focused on trying to find any existing documentation/research/wireframes/mockups that may be able to help target inconsistencies, walking through the application on target platforms to understand and map out flows/inconsistencies, and look at competitors to understand what best practices appear to be across streaming services. This resulted in the following:

  • Metadata Analysis and Usability Analysis: To start tackling the consistency issues across platforms, I started off by conducting a metadata analysis for FAST/AVOD. The metadata analysis consisted of walking through each major page within the FAST/AVOD flows, wireframing them out completely (paying extra attention to the formatting and display of metadata), and then walking through to see the best way to standardize formatting across platforms. I also was able to use these wireframes to map out feature/usability inconsistencies and recommendations around them. Part of the flows for TV FAST apps is shown to the right.
  • Competitive Analysis: While looking through competitors, I conducted a few analyses centered around the following: metadata formatting across FAST/AVOD, general structure/flow of FAST/AVOD, and general page organization. For these analyses, I maintained a list of 11 competitors (5 direct, 6 indirect) for AVOD and a list of between 10-15 competitors for FAST (this varied based on users and functionality being considered). Direct competitors were streaming services that were free ad-supported services, indirect competitors were paid streaming services.

User Research

With the information collected from the non-user research, I wrote up a test plan for usability research across FAST/AVOD platforms.

  • For the FAST study: The target users were initially focused around non-PMS (personal media server) users that watched at least 1-2 hours of live TV on a weekly basis, not specifically on Plex, but in general. Mobile, web, and TV platforms were targetted for this study. Users were asked to walk through the way they would typically use FAST, describing anything they liked/disliked/etc and then were asked questions around their experience, their watching habits, etc at the end to understand user behavior and content consumption. The study initially had over 80 participants, with about 40-50 usable studies used for research analysis as the study was unmoderated through UserTesting.com.
  • For the AVOD study: The target users were focused around non-PMS (personal media server) users that watched at least 1-2 hours of live TV on a weekly basis, not specifically on Plex, but in general. Mobile, web, and TV platforms were targetted for this study. Users were asked to walk through the way they would typically use AVOD, describing anything they liked/disliked/etc and then were asked questions around their experience, their watching habits, etc at the end to understand user behavior and content consumption. The study initially had over 110 participants, with a little over 60 usable studies used for research analysis as the study was unmoderated through UserTesting.com.

Recommendations and Brainstorming

With all the research conducted, I put together an synthesis of my findings, also including in a JTBD persona for each study as requested by my product team. After presenting the synthesis to my product team as well as various other stakeholders, I was able to put together a brainstorming workshop to use with my stakeholders to generate new feature ideas for FAST and AVOD. From the brainstorming workshops, I took the most popular ideas and put them into a prioritization matrix and eventually a UX strategy/research/design backlog for future iterations of each flow. AVOD Brainstorming Workshop

The FAST and AVOD brainstorming workshops were formatted similarly with affinity mapping, creative matrices, and voting on ideas. Specifically for the AVOD workshop, participants started with a warm-up activity to step into the shoes of the user by recalling as many titles from the main pages of Plex as possible. We then moved onto affinity mapping, where participants were sorted through existing challenges they saw with AVOD (through personal experience and user research), dividing them into groups and then, as a group, coming up with How Might We statements based off of them. From there, we used those HMW statements for our creative matrix and then voted on items via Monopoly money. Shown below are the affinity mapping and creative matrix activities.

AVOD Prioritization Session

Items from the AVOD workshop were pulled into the matrix for prioritization. Following the initial session, I broke out the items into different sections of where research was needed, what we needed more info on in general, what were mainly dev-focused items, what were items were already got started on from a design perspective, and miscellaous, sorting each column by priority order.
