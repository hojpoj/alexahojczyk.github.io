Fifth Third Bank - External API Portal

Project Overview

Within Enterprise applications at Fifth Third, I led all user experience and user interfaces efforts over the past year within several major projects. One of these projects was aimed at creating an external facing API portal for the bank. During this time, we conducted major competitive and user research around API portals and API integration. The end product was a fully fleshed out release plan with three phases, wireframes, site maps, style guides, branding resources, etc supplied for each phase.

Target Users

Fintech developers/developers looking to leverage a banking API

Fintech upper management looking to leverage a banking API

Deliverables Created

  • Low fidelity wireframes created in Sketch
  • User research plans
  • User research synthesis report
  • Competitive analysis of major API portals on the market
  • User personas for major user groups
  • Style guide and design system assets
  • Accessibility recommendations/considerations

Main Challenges

  • Because of the change in priority, we only had a limited amount of time to get a full plan/blueprint developer-ready within 2/3 months of starting
  • The first round of user interviews focused around director-level individuals within development; the second round of interviews intended to be developer-focused were not allotted within our time restrictions
  • The tools and frameworks used to develop the portal were very restrictive in terms of functionality that would to the best UX and UI

Design Process

Below is a snapshot of each step of the process conducted in order to complete this project.

  • Requirements Gathering

    Meeting with stakeholders to understand problem and business/product needs

  • Non-User Research

    Competitive analyses, heuristic analyses, metadata analyses, etc

  • User Research

    Qualitative user interviews to walk through and evaluate current experiences with banking APIs and needs/want with banking APIS

  • Synthesis & Recommendations

    Putting everything together to form the whole picture and where to go

  • Wireframing /Blueprinting

    Iterative wireframing to establish flows and functionality across platforms

The Full Process

Requirements Gathering

For this project, I was approached by one of my enterprise product teams with the desire of wanting to build an API portal for others to leverage. This would be the first externally facing product the enterprise team would have ever implemented. The target users initially were developers implementing products/applications that would require leveraging a banking API (for example, if a product wanted to leverage certain transactional functionality, they would be able to go to this portal in order to call the API and build it into their application). As far as other requirements went, it was fairly open-ended with just the general idea of an API portal.

Non-User Research

As the project was fairly ambiguous, my first step was to look through competitor portals to see what was generally involved with the design and usability of a banking API portal. I targetted several different portals and was able to establish a general structure most of them followed as well as what each of them offered in terms of information/functionality. As I moved through each competitor, it did bring up a question we hadn't considered before which was if we wanted to put up some kind of registration gate. For some competitors, they utilized a registration gate to hide all of their API documentation, while others didn't and just left the information open to the public.

While going through competitor portals, I was having discussions with my developers around what they had been looking at as reference and discovered that we did have a pre-existing API portal that we had never pushed to the public. With access to that portal, I did my own heuristic analysis to see where usability could be improved as well as worked towards comparing our pre-exisiting portal to the competitors analyzed previously.

User Research, Synthesis, & Recommendations

Initially, there were no plans to interview users as the original timeline was much tighter than what it was adjusted to being. User interviews were introduced once we discovered we had more time as the API was being written (and because I bugged my product team to let me as I wanted to gather more information on functionality and previous experiences before continuing with a design).

  • The first round of user interviews were conducted virtually in-person with 10 different CEOs/CTOS/engineering managers from different companies that were partnered with Fifth Third. They were recruited via the product team after explaining the concept we were planning on designing out. Interviews were 60 minutes sessions focused around previous experiences, expectations for an API portal, components they liked/disliked from portals they've used/their devs have used, what they were hoping for with this new portal, etc.
  • The second round of user interviews were also conducted virtually in-person with 10 different software engineers who had experience actually implementing banking APIs in products they had either currently or previously worked on. Similar to the first round of interviews, these users were 60 minutes sessions focused around previous experiences, expectations for an API portal, components they liked/disliked from portals they've used/their devs have used, what they were hoping for with this new portal, etc, though with some more technically oriented questions that would be more relevant to them.

From these interviews, I was able to synthesize my results and present my findings to my product and engineering teams, providing what I recommended as the direction we go in with the portal. We all came to a consensus on the direction and moved forward to the next steps.

Wireframing /Blueprinting

With enough insights, I started off by creating a low fidelity version of the screens for the application as well as a site map of those screens. With those screens designed, I then created flows for each phase we had decided on for the portal, adding to the site map to show what gets added where during each phase. The site map also acted as the information architecture for the layout of the API portal.

Because of a shift in priorities for myself as well as key engineering resources, there were no further design iterations for this project. I ended up delivering a fully fleshed out release plan with three phases, wireframes, site maps, style guides, branding resources, etc supplied for each phase to my developers with the short time left I had to dedicate to the project before moving on. I also made sure they knew they could always message me with design questions as the portal got implemented, I just couldn't be a dedicated resource anymore as other projects had higher priority.

AVOD Brainstorming Workshop

The FAST and AVOD brainstorming workshops were formatted similarly with affinity mapping, creative matrices, and voting on ideas. Specifically for the AVOD workshop, participants started with a warm-up activity to step into the shoes of the user by recalling as many titles from the main pages of Plex as possible. We then moved onto affinity mapping, where participants were sorted through existing challenges they saw with AVOD (through personal experience and user research), dividing them into groups and then, as a group, coming up with How Might We statements based off of them. From there, we used those HMW statements for our creative matrix and then voted on items via Monopoly money. Shown below are the affinity mapping and creative matrix activities.

AVOD Prioritization Session

Items from the AVOD workshop were pulled into the matrix for prioritization. Following the initial session, I broke out the items into different sections of where research was needed, what we needed more info on in general, what were mainly dev-focused items, what were items were already got started on from a design perspective, and miscellaous, sorting each column by priority order.
