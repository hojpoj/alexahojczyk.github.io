Design Process

In general for most projects, I try to follow some version of the double diamond approach with four phases of design: discover, define, design, deliver. Obviously because of the ever-changing nature of each project, it's not always possible to perform these steps in order or even at all under very restrictive processes/deadlines, but ideally, this is how I like to take my approach in order to make sure design is at the beginning of the process as well as keep the user involved/in mind during ever step as well.

  • Discover
    • Stakeholder interviews
    • Market analysis
    • Competitive analysis
    • Resource gathering
    • Research
  • Define
    • Problem definition
    • Design blueprint
    • Roadmapping/MVP definition
    • Brainstorming
    • User flows/journey mapping
  • Design
    • Wireframing
    • High/Mid fidelity mockups
    • Interative prototypes
    • Prototype testing
    • Rapid Prototyping
  • Deliver
    • Final mockups/prototypes
    • Styleguides
    • Final research
    • Design deliverables

My Key Principles

Most designers/UXers (hopefully) follow the same design process, but we do have different guiding principles and elements that are personally important to us as we work towards solutions. The key principles that I try to integrate into any design process that I execute are:

Proactive, Not Reactive

We’ve heard this expression multiple times, but as I’ve progressed in my career, I have always worked towards staying as far ahead as possible. I work to make sure that my team and I are constantly considering what comes next after we complete current tasks to make sure we’re on top of the game and we’re already tackling the next problem instead of trying to panic and solve it last minute with less than desirable parameters.

Informed, Not Ignorant

A principle that applies to everything, not just research, I try to stay as informed as possible in every step of the process to make the best decisions possible. Staying informed through research/data, design progress, engineering progress, product needs, communication, etc is key to making sure I have enough information while working on complex systems to know which direction to head in next.

Universal, Not (Just) Inclusive

Accessibility has always been of key importance to me from the very first job I has as a front end software engineer/UX designer. While I love when people practice any kind of accessibility, I love to incorporate universal design principles rather than just inclusive design principles as my personal belief is that if you’re designing according to best practices, you’ll be creating one solution that works for all your users instead of having to design an unnecessary amount of additional solutions that aim to correct what was missing from the first solution
